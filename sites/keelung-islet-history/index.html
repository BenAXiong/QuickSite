Why this tiny island mattered

Keelung Islet is small, but historically it was never trivial. Its offshore position made it a maritime marker, a defensive outwork for the Keelung area, a military space for long stretches, and later a tightly managed public destination after years of closure and repair.

Strategic logicApproach marker for the Keelung coast and harbor zone.
Military legacyRestricted and used for training / defense functions before later tourism opening.
Modern iconThe lighthouse and volcanic silhouette made it a visual emblem of Keelung’s seafront.
Recovery storyStorm damage led to a multi-year closure before reopening in 2019.

Why ports care about islands like this

Ports and militaries often value offshore islands less as places to live and more as places to see from, orient by, monitor around, and hold as defensive depth.

Lighthouse and late-20th-century symbolism

The present lighthouse dates to 1980 and helps explain why the island remains so legible in the public imagination: it is not just a piece of rock offshore, but a marked point of orientation with a clean graphic identity.

1980 milestone

The current lighthouse strengthened the island’s modern identity as a recognized navigational point and visual symbol.

Solar-powered operation

Later summaries describe the light as solar-powered, reinforcing the island’s small-footprint, autonomous feel.

Black-and-white iconography

The lighthouse’s striped look made it photographically potent and helped bridge maritime utility with tourism branding.
