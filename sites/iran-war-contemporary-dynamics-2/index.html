The picture in one screen

The risk is not just battlefield intensity. It is the collision of four systems: direct state fire, proxy adaptation, maritime chokepoints, and a nuclear file still clouded by damaged facilities and incomplete verification.

01 · direct fire

Iran and Israel are no longer hiding fully behind deniability.

The line from covert shadow conflict to overt state-on-state missile exchange was crossed in 2024 and deepened in the 12-day war of June 2025. The 2026 round pushes that logic further.

02 · proxy adaptation

Iran’s network is damaged, not gone.

Hezbollah has taken severe losses and its Syria supply route was cut after Assad’s fall, but Iran still retains partner forces, missile capacity, and the ability to create multi-front pressure.

03 · energy coercion

Hormuz turns a regional war into a global market shock.

Even partial disruption hits oil, LNG, shipping insurance, freight patterns, and Asian importers. That is why coercion in the Gulf has outsized strategic weight.

04 · nuclear opacity

Bomb damage does not equal political clarity.

IAEA reporting before and during the 2025 war highlighted large 60%-enriched stockpiles and stressed that inspectors need access to re-establish facts on the ground after strikes.

How the system changed

The contemporary war environment only makes sense if you read it as a sequence of escalations, shocks, and degraded deterrence.

APR 2024

Iran launches its first direct attack on Israel.

Reuters reported more than 300 drones and missiles in the April 13–14 attack, a turning point because Tehran moved from mainly proxy warfare to openly acknowledged retaliation.

DEC 2024

Assad falls, and the Syria corridor fractures.

CFR and Reuters both highlighted that the collapse of Assad’s regime removed a critical logistical node for Iran and cut Hezbollah’s overland supply route through Syria.

JUN 2025

The 12-day Iran–Israel war resets the baseline.

Israel struck Iranian nuclear and missile targets on June 13; the U.S. later attacked key nuclear sites; a U.S.-backed ceasefire followed on June 24. The old “managed shadow war” baseline was gone.

FEB–MAR 2026

Renewed war spreads leverage to infrastructure and shipping.

Reuters now describes direct strikes on Iranian missile infrastructure, continued Iranian missile fire, renewed Hezbollah escalation, and threats centered on Hormuz plus Gulf energy and water assets.

Where pressure sits now

Tap a lens to narrow the map. The key point: the war is not one front. It is an interacting stack of military, political, maritime, and verification crises.

Iran homeland

Iran is absorbing direct attacks on missile and nuclear-linked infrastructure while still trying to preserve retaliatory credibility. That creates strong incentives for symbolic and disruptive counterstrikes, even when its broader regional position is weaker than before.

  • Leadership must show resilience without inviting total collapse.
  • Infrastructure targeting raises pressure to widen the target set.
  • Domestic damage increases regime sensitivity to prestige and deterrence.
high civilian exposure air defense stress counterforce logic

Israel front

Iranian fire still aims to prove that interception is not perfect and that strategic disruption remains possible. Even limited missile penetration has outsized psychological and political effect because the war now plays out in dense urban areas and around high-value military nodes.

  • Air defense success is real, but not absolute.
  • Strikes on population centers create escalation pressure.
  • Israel is trying to degrade launch capacity faster than Iran can signal staying power.
severe attrition IRGC rebuild help fragmented command

Lebanon / Hezbollah

Hezbollah is no longer the intact high-end deterrent it once was, but Reuters reports an IRGC-led restructuring after the 2024 war and a shift toward smaller decentralized units. That means lower elegance, not zero danger.

  • Leadership and logistics took heavy hits.
  • Decentralized cells can still sustain attritional pressure.
  • Lebanon’s civilian infrastructure is increasingly exposed.
post-Assad gap corridor degraded militia residue

Iraq / Syria corridor

The fall of Assad changed the geometry of Iranian power projection. The overland route into Lebanon became far less reliable, yet Iraq-based armed actors and residual networks still matter for launch depth, logistics, and coercive signaling.

  • Syria is no longer a secure Iranian rear area.
  • That weakens Hezbollah resupply and IRGC mobility.
  • But it does not erase Iraq-based militant leverage.
global market lever shipping insurance Asian exposure

Gulf / Hormuz

This is Iran’s most globally scalable lever. Closing or selectively coercing traffic through Hormuz can impose costs far beyond the battlefield, especially on Asian energy importers and LNG buyers.

  • Roughly one-fifth of global oil and LNG flows remain exposed.
  • Alternative pipeline capacity only offsets part of the shock.
  • Threats to desalination and energy assets widen the regional risk map.
verification gap damaged sites diplomacy hinge

Nuclear file

The hardest analytical question is not simply “how much was destroyed?” but “what can be verified now?” IAEA statements in 2025 stressed both the size of Iran’s 60%-enriched stockpile and the necessity of renewed inspector access after strikes.

  • Physical damage can slow programs without settling intent.
  • Material accounting becomes more important after war.
  • Diplomacy without verification will look thin; verification without diplomacy will struggle to stick.

Bottom line

The contemporary war is not mainly about territory. It is about deterrence credibility, survivable strike capacity, chokepoint leverage, and the political meaning of an only partly visible nuclear file.

Three plausible next paths

Not probabilities. Think of these as strategic shapes. The first is the easiest to imagine, the second is the most economically violent, and the third is the hardest to construct but the only one that meaningfully cools the nuclear dimension.

Path A

Rolling limited war

Direct strikes continue, but below the threshold of decisive state collapse or full Hormuz closure. The conflict stays expensive, politically combustible, and regionally contagious without fully exploding.
